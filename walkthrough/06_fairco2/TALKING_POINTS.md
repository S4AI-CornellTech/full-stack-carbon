# Segment 6 — Fair-CO2: who pays for the carbon?

**Story beat.** The payoff. The server's embodied carbon (segments 1 & 5) and its
operational energy don't bill themselves — they must be **fairly attributed** to the
software sharing the machine. Fair-CO2 does that for co-located workloads.

**Run it:** `make demo-fairco2` — reproduces Fair-CO2's colocation figures from its
committed artifact-evaluation matrices (no cluster, no live workloads).

**Headline — attributed embodied carbon (gCO2e):**

| Workload | run alone | co-located with the other |
|---|---|---|
| Llama-3-8B | 0.161 | **0.080** |
| Spark | 0.293 | **0.168** |

Co-locating the two roughly **halves** each job's attributed embodied carbon — they
share the same physical hardware over overlapping time, so neither should be charged
for all of it.

**Figures:**
- `fairco2_attribution.png` — the table above as grouped bars.
- `2a_runtime_relative_change_matrix_new.pdf`, `2b_proportional_energy_relative_change_matrix_new.pdf`
  — Fair-CO2's own colocation-interference heatmaps, regenerated by its own script.

**What to say (slide bullets):**
- Embodied carbon is a *shared, fixed* cost; attributing it fairly across tenants is
  the open problem Fair-CO2 tackles (RUP baseline vs Shapley-value ground truth).
- The server's embodied budget here is **ACT-derived**: Fair-CO2 hardcodes a per-CPU
  constant of 18,530 gCO2e (`colocation/process_colocation_sweep.py:43`) — exactly the
  kind of number segment 1 produces. This is where the chain closes.
- The whole result is recomputed from committed intermediate data — the
  artifact-evaluation pattern the entire walkthrough follows.

**Closes the loop with:**
- Segment 1 (ACT) R740 embodied ≈ 1,523 kgCO2e · Segment 5 (EServe) H100 ≈ 154 kgCO2e
  — the hardware whose carbon is being attributed to software here.
